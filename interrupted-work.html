The Cost of Interrupted Work

Gloria Mark's Groundbreaking Research on Workplace Interruptions

Dr. Gloria Mark - UC Irvine Department of Informatics
Published in Proceedings of CHI 2008

The Research Background

2008

Dr. Gloria Mark and her team published landmark research that fundamentally changed how we understand workplace interruptions.

Their study provided the first empirical evidence of what many knowledge workers intuitively felt: constant interruptions are taking a serious toll on both productivity and well-being.

The Research Question

Does the context of an interruption matter?

Mark's study aimed to resolve conflicting ideas about interruptions in the workplace. Previous research had produced mixed results—some studies suggested interruptions could be beneficial, while others described them as harmful.

Specifically, the researchers wanted to understand:
🎯
Context Impact
Whether interruptions related to your current task are less disruptive than unrelated ones
😰
Stress Effects
How interrupted work affects stress levels and mental state
👤
Individual Factors
What personality traits might influence how well people handle interruptions

The Experimental Design

The researchers created a controlled office environment in their lab and used a realistic email task. Participants played the role of human resource managers who had just returned from vacation.

👥
Participants
48 individuals
• 81% German university students
• Average age: 26
• 15 males, 33 females
• All experienced email users
📧
Task
Process 12 emails per condition
• As quickly and accurately as possible
• Human resources context
• Fact sheet provided for reference
• Equal difficulty across conditions
⏰
Interruptions
Every 2 minutes
• Based on real workplace observations
• Via phone or instant message
• Participants must respond immediately
• Equal duration across conditions

The Three Experimental Conditions

B
Baseline

No interruptions - participants could focus entirely on processing emails

S
Same-Context

Interruptions with questions related to human resources (matching their email task)

D
Different-Context

Interruptions with random, unrelated questions (like planning a company party)

🤯 The Surprising Results

The findings overturned conventional wisdom about interruptions and revealed unexpected patterns in human behavior.

Finding #1
Context Doesn't Matter
The most surprising discovery: the context of interruptions made no difference. Whether the interruption was related to the current task or completely random, the disruption cost was identical.
Finding #2
People Work Faster When Interrupted
Contrary to expectations, participants actually completed interrupted tasks faster than uninterrupted ones. The baseline (no interruption) condition took the longest time to complete.
Finding #3
Speed Comes at a Price
While people worked faster when interrupted, this came with significant psychological costs: increased stress, frustration, time pressure, and mental effort.
Finding #4
Quality Remained Intact
Despite working faster and feeling more stressed, participants maintained the same quality of work. No significant differences in errors, politeness, or accuracy.

The Psychological Mechanism

The Speed-Stress Spiral

Mark's research revealed a fascinating adaptation strategy: when people expect to be interrupted, they develop a compensatory working mode.

1

Anticipate Interruptions

People expect to be disrupted during work

2

Work Faster

They compensate by increasing their work pace

3

Create Stress

Increased pace creates psychological stress

4

Cycle Continues

The pattern repeats, building cumulative stress

Individual Differences: Who Handles Interruptions Better?

The study uncovered important personality factors that influence interruption tolerance.

🌟 Openness to Experience

People who scored higher on "openness to experience" handled interrupted work more efficiently.

These individuals are better at:
  • Adapting quickly to new situations
  • Handling unexpected tasks
  • Reorienting back to original work faster
📋 Need for Personal Structure

Surprisingly, people with a higher need for personal structure also performed better with interruptions.

These individuals are better at:
  • Managing their time when disrupted
  • Organizing work around interruptions
  • Creating mental frameworks to cope

Real-World Implications

⚠️ The 20-Minute Warning

After just 20 minutes of interrupted work, participants reported significantly higher stress, frustration, and mental effort.

11
minutes between interruptions in real workplaces
23
minutes to fully refocus after interruption
20
minutes to show measurable stress effects
🔄 The Productivity Paradox
While people can work faster when interrupted, this speed comes at the cost of increased stress, higher mental workload, greater time pressure, and more physical and emotional effort.
💻 System Design Insights
Technology should help track and control interruptions over time, provide individual customization based on personality differences, and give people visibility into their interruption patterns.
🧠 Cognitive Load Management
Any discontinuity in work creates cognitive costs. Anticipation of interruption changes how people work, and short-term adaptation doesn't eliminate long-term psychological costs.

Study Limitations & Context

🏢
Laboratory Setting
May not fully replicate real workplace conditions and social dynamics
📧
Task Specificity
Email task may not represent all types of knowledge work and cognitive demands
⏱️
Time Frame
Short-term effects (20 minutes) may not predict long-term adaptation patterns
🌍
Cultural Factors
German university student population may limit generalizability to other cultures

🎯 Key Takeaways for Modern Workers

1
All interruptions have costs - it doesn't matter if they're related to your work or not. Any break in focus creates cognitive disruption.
2
Speed compensation is real - you can work faster when interrupted, but you'll pay a psychological price in stress and mental effort.
3
Stress accumulates quickly - even 20 minutes of interrupted work significantly increases stress, frustration, and time pressure.
4
Individual differences matter - your personality affects how well you handle interruptions. Openness and structure preference both help.
5
System-level solutions are needed - personal willpower alone isn't enough. We need tools and strategies to manage interruption costs.

Applications to Modern Productivity Systems

Mark's findings provide the scientific foundation for productivity strategies like day theming, batching, and focused work periods.

📅 Day Theming
Grouping similar tasks reduces context switching costs. Since all interruptions are equally disruptive, protecting entire blocks of time becomes crucial.
⏰ Batching Strategies
Processing emails, messages, and calls in dedicated blocks prevents the speed-stress spiral from occurring throughout the day.
🔕 Notification Management
Since anticipation of interruption changes how people work, controlling when interruptions can occur is essential for maintaining focus.
🎯 Deep Work Periods
Protecting uninterrupted time allows for the natural working pace without the psychological costs of speed compensation.
👤 Personal Customization
Individual differences in openness and structure preference suggest that interruption management strategies should be personalized.
📊 Stress Monitoring
Since stress accumulates quickly with interruptions, tracking and managing psychological load becomes as important as task completion.

The Revolutionary Impact

Gloria Mark's research provided the first rigorous scientific evidence of something many workers experience daily: interruptions exact a real cost on our mental well-being, even when we can maintain productivity.

"Our most profound insight: our brains are remarkably adaptable, capable of working faster when interrupted, but this adaptation comes with an invisible tax of increased stress and mental effort."

Understanding this trade-off is crucial for anyone seeking to optimize their work environment and mental health in our increasingly connected world.
